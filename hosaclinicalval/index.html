⏳ Follow-Up Meeting Scheduled

An in-person follow-up meeting with Nathaniel Bleker is pending. The feedback below was received in writing prior to that meeting and reflects his initial detailed technical review.

Key Engineering Feedback - Summary

Hardware Architecture
Recommends eliminating the standalone hardware unit and running the system on an overhead-mounted phone using its wide-angle camera. Advises removing 2D barcodes (ArUco markers) in favor of object segmentation and eliminating any vertical obstacles a limb could accidentally contact.
FDA Regulatory Strategy
The FDA does not yet have established in-house expertise for evaluating LLMs and AI in investigational device exemption submissions - expect extensive questioning and costly testing. Recommends carefully planning the regulatory approach early. His own IDE submission for an implantable stimulator was 11,000 pages of compliance documentation.
Software Architecture
Core requirement: one high-resolution wide-angle camera, a speaker, and a well-trained model. Audio feedback is essential - people will not watch a screen in a surgical environment.
Quality Management System (QMS)
Device development must occur under a formal QMS including SOPs, software development plans, architecture documents, risk analyses, and failure mode enumeration. The FDA requires documentation of effectively every decision made during development.
Critical Engineering Decisions Ahead
1. Produce own hardware, or use COTS (commercial off-the-shelf)? 2. Identify a usability partner with surgical suite experience. 3. Define a field support and repair strategy - including whether the device must survive sterilization.
Human Factors
Human response time, average hearing and vision of OR staff, ambient noise levels, and limb movement speed in the surgical theater must all inform the design. Recommends reviewing FDA guidance on applying human factors engineering to medical devices.

Full Letter

To Whom It May Concern, I am writing in strong support of SterileZone Guardian, an AI-powered sterile field monitoring system developed by Suhas Chinta and his team at Keller Collegiate Academy. Having reviewed the technical and clinical documentation accompanying this project, I am impressed by both the rigor of the team's approach and the genuine clinical relevance of the problem they are addressing. Surgical site infections remain a significant and largely preventable burden on the healthcare system, and the absence of any automated, objective monitoring solution in current clinical practice represents a meaningful gap. SterileZone Guardian addresses this gap directly. The system's core capabilities - real-time boundary breach detection at greater than 99% accuracy, sub-100-millisecond response latency, millisecond-accurate event logging, and a self-contained hardware form factor requiring no network dependency or workflow disruption - reflect a level of technical and clinical thinking that is exceptional for any innovator, and particularly remarkable for a student team. From a clinical standpoint, I find the event logging capability to be among the most compelling features of this system. The ability to generate a permanent, auditable compliance record of sterile field activity addresses a long-standing limitation in both quality assurance and training environments, where accountability has historically depended entirely on real-time human observation. I endorse SterileZone Guardian as a clinically meaningful, technically sound innovation with a credible development roadmap and genuine potential for impact across operating rooms, simulation laboratories, and sterile manufacturing environments. I commend Suhas and his team for the seriousness and professionalism with which they have pursued this work, and I wish them every success at HOSA Medical Innovation Internationals. Please do not hesitate to contact me should you require any further information. Sincerely, Dr. Vikaskumar Patel DDS, MBA, BDS, FICOI, AFAAID, FAGD Olney Dental Wellness

Full Letter - May 15, 2026

To Whom It May Concern, I am pleased to provide this letter in support of the student team competing in the HOSA Medical Innovation International event with their project focused on the development of an artificial intelligence (AI)-based Sterile Field Monitor. As a medical device manufacturer, I see expanded value beyond the initial vision to monitor the sterile boundary. Loss of sterility to packaged devices can introduce infections that are more likely to lead to sepsis. Current inspection practices rely heavily on manual visual inspection by operating room personnel, which may be subject to variability due to time constraints, environmental conditions, and human factors. Development of automated optical inspection technologies is an area of growing interest within the medical device industry, particularly for applications involving defect detection, seal inspection, and packaging quality evaluation. Exploring these technologies in the context of point-of-use inspection within the operating room is highly relevant. The proposed application of AI-assisted optical monitoring has the potential to improve consistency, support clinical decision-making, and enhance patient safety by providing an additional layer of quality assessment - not only for monitoring the sterile boundary, but also when applied immediately before a product is introduced into the sterile field. What makes this project particularly impressive is the team's ability to combine concepts from healthcare, biomedical engineering, artificial intelligence, and quality assurance into a practical and clinically relevant application. Their work reflects a strong understanding of both the technical and real-world considerations associated with the operating room. In addition to the technical aspects of the project, the students have demonstrated initiative, creativity, professionalism, and a genuine interest in solving meaningful healthcare problems. The project exemplifies the type of interdisciplinary thinking and innovation encouraged through HOSA's Medical Innovation program. I strongly support this team and believe their project represents a thoughtful and promising contribution to healthcare technology innovation. I commend their dedication, curiosity, and commitment to improving patient care through engineering and scientific problem solving. Regards, Holle Carey Sr. Regulatory Engineer University of Texas at Dallas - Texas Biomedical Device Center
